Short answer

You can make many visa photos with an iPhone, but the important part is not the camera — it is matching the exact document rules. A visa photo may need 2 × 2 inches, 35 × 45 mm, 33 × 48 mm, or another size depending on the country and visa type. The safest workflow is to confirm the official requirement first, take a plain well-lit portrait, crop it precisely, and only then print or upload. Snapport is useful when you want an iPhone workflow for passport, visa, and ID photo preparation rather than guessing in a generic photo editor.

The honest caveat: no app can guarantee acceptance if the underlying photo breaks government rules. Treat the app as a sizing and preparation helper, then compare the result with the official checklist before submitting. That is especially important for visas, where embassies may enforce rules differently from passport offices.

What to look for before choosing

  • Use official size rules for the specific passport, visa, ID card, or baby document.
  • Check background, head size, face position, shadows, glasses, and recency rules before paying or printing.
  • Prefer an app that lets you prepare the photo privately on iPhone and export a printable result.
  • Do not rely on beauty filters or AI retouching; official document photos are judged on accuracy, not style.
  • If a physical print is required, confirm photo paper, DPI, and whether the office accepts home prints.

A practical decision process

  1. Start with the country and document type: passport, visa, residence card, or school ID can use different dimensions.
  2. Take the photo in bright even light: face the camera, keep a neutral expression, and avoid shadows behind the head.
  3. Crop to the exact size: use the required millimeters, inches, or 300 DPI pixel dimensions.
  4. Run a final checklist: background, head height, eye line, no filters, and recent photo date.
  5. Export or print only after checking the official government page for the latest rule.
